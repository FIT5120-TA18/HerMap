Employment scams to watch out for

work_alert

A job should help your finances, not put them at risk

When you are 18–22, studying, working casually, or looking for flexible work, a “quick money” job can look tempting. But some fake jobs are designed to steal your money, your personal information, or access to your bank account.

1. “Pay first to get the job”

Be careful if someone asks you to pay for training, registration, equipment, starter kits, background checks, or software before you can begin. A real job should not require you to pay money just to earn money.

2. “High pay, low effort” jobs

Scams often promise easy money for simple tasks like liking posts, reviewing products, receiving parcels, or doing “remote admin” with no proper interview. If it sounds too easy for the pay offered, pause and check.

3. Requests for personal documents too early

Be cautious if a stranger asks for your passport, licence, Tax File Number, bank details, or Medicare details before you have verified the employer and the role. These details can be used for identity theft.

4. Using your bank account for transfers

Never agree to receive money and send it somewhere else for a “job”. This could involve stolen money or money laundering, even if the person makes it sound normal.

shield

Before accepting a job, check the company website, search the recruiter’s name, avoid WhatsApp-only hiring, do not pay upfront, and never share sensitive documents unless you are sure the job is real.

ABS Data Sources

The numbers and benchmarks used across this website are based on datasets from the Australian Bureau of Statistics, commonly known as the ABS.

The ABS is Australia's national statistical agency. It collects and publishes official data about population, housing, work, income, cost of living, and other areas that help explain how people live across Australia.

In Hermap, ABS data is used in context to support comparisons around living costs, income, location, and financial readiness. These figures are intended to help users better understand possible trade-offs, not to provide personal financial advice.
